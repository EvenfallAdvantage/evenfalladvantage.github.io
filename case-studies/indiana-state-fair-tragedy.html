Indiana State Fair Stage Collapse

2011 Weather Emergency

For Unarmed Event Security Training
Date of Incident: August 13, 2011
Location: Indiana State Fairgrounds, Indianapolis, Indiana
Event: Indiana State Fair (Sugarland Concert)
Prepared by: James "Fergie" Ferguson
Last Updated: February 28, 2025

Overview

On August 13, 2011, a severe weather event caused the temporary outdoor concert stage at the Indiana State Fair to collapse, resulting in seven deaths and nearly 100 injuries. The tragedy occurred minutes before a scheduled performance by the country duo Sugarland as a powerful storm front approached the venue. Despite weather warnings received more than an hour before the collapse, event organizers failed to evacuate the venue, highlighting critical failures in weather monitoring, emergency decision-making, and communication protocols. This case study examines the incident from the perspective of unarmed security personnel to identify key lessons in emergency preparedness and crowd safety management.

Background

Event Details: The Indiana State Fair is an annual multi-week event that draws large crowds. The 2011 fair included a series of concerts at the Hoosier Lottery Grandstand, an outdoor venue with temporary stage structures. On the night of the incident, approximately 10,000 spectators were gathered for the Sugarland concert.

Security Staffing: The event employed a mix of Indiana State Police, local law enforcement, private security firms, and fair volunteers. Unarmed security personnel were primarily tasked with crowd control, access management, and general public safety.

Weather Conditions: The National Weather Service had issued severe thunderstorm watches and warnings for the area earlier in the day, with forecasts predicting strong winds exceeding 60mph.

Timeline of Events

  • 5:30-6:00 PM: The National Weather Service issues a severe thunderstorm watch for the area including the fairgrounds.
  • 7:00 PM: Opening act performs on schedule despite increasingly concerning weather forecasts.
  • 8:00 PM: Weather radar clearly shows a severe storm approaching Indianapolis with anticipated arrival around 8:30-9:00 PM.
  • 8:39 PM: National Weather Service issues a severe thunderstorm warning specifically for the fairgrounds area.
  • 8:45-8:50 PM: A private weather forecaster contacts fair officials, warning of severe weather with winds of 60-70mph expected to hit within 30 minutes.
  • 8:45-8:57 PM: Various discussions occur among fair officials, Sugarland's management, and security personnel about whether to delay the show. No evacuation is ordered.
  • 8:46 PM: A fair official makes an announcement acknowledging the weather but only suggesting that attendees might seek shelter if desired while assuring them the concert would proceed.
  • 8:58 PM: Security personnel observe dark clouds and visible lightning approaching.
  • 9:00 PM: A gust front with winds measured at 59-70 mph hits the venue. The temporary roof structure and rigging above the stage collapses into the crowd.
  • Aftermath: Seven people are killed, and 58 are hospitalized with injuries ranging from cuts and broken bones to critical head trauma. Dozens more receive on-site medical treatment.

Key Failures in Security and Event Management

Inadequate Weather Monitoring and Response:

  • No designated weather authority with clear decision-making power was established for the event.
  • Despite receiving multiple severe weather warnings, officials failed to act decisively.
  • No predetermined wind-speed thresholds were established for mandatory evacuation.

Poorly Defined Chain of Command:

  • Multiple entities (fair officials, production company, security firms, state police) had overlapping responsibilities with unclear authority boundaries.
  • No single individual was empowered to order an evacuation, resulting in decision paralysis.
  • Security personnel lacked clear protocols for escalating safety concerns to decision-makers.

Insufficient Emergency Planning:

  • The emergency action plan existed but was vague about weather emergencies and evacuation procedures.
  • Staff received minimal training on the emergency plan, with many security personnel unaware of their specific roles during evacuation.
  • Evacuation routes and sheltering locations were not clearly communicated to staff or attendees.

Ineffective Communication Systems:

  • No unified communication system existed between weather monitors, event officials, and security personnel.
  • The public address system was inadequate for emergency communications, both in volume and clarity.
  • Security personnel lacked consistent radio protocols for emergency situations.

Structural Vulnerabilities:

  • The temporary stage structure was not designed to withstand the wind loads specified in building codes.
  • No engineering review of the stage construction was conducted specific to severe weather risks.
  • Security personnel were not trained to recognize or report potentially dangerous structural issues.

Impact

  • Human Toll: Seven deaths and nearly 100 injuries, many severe and life-altering.
  • Legal Consequences: Multiple lawsuits resulted in settlements exceeding $50 million. The Indiana State Fair Commission, producers, and stage companies all faced litigation.
  • Industry Changes: The incident led to significant reforms in outdoor event safety standards nationwide, including stricter requirements for temporary structures and mandatory severe weather action plans.

Lessons for Unarmed Event Security

Weather Awareness and Reporting:

  • Monitor weather conditions actively, including watching for visual indicators of severe weather (wall clouds, lightning, sudden wind shifts).
  • Understand basic severe weather terminology (watch vs. warning) and the implications for event safety.
  • Maintain communication with supervisors about changing weather conditions, especially those that concern attendees.

Emergency Response Readiness:

  • Know evacuation routes and sheltering locations before an emergency occurs.
  • Understand your specific role during evacuations, including assigned areas and populations to assist.
  • Practice clear, concise communication of emergency instructions to the public.
At the Indiana State Fair, security personnel found themselves in an impossible position: they could see the danger approaching but lacked both the authority to act independently and a clear channel to prompt action from decision-makers. This disconnect between observation and response capability represents one of the most critical failures in event safety management.

Recommendations for Event Organizers

  • Clear Weather Authority: Designate a specific individual with meteorological expertise and unilateral authority to order show delays or evacuations based on predefined criteria.
  • Decision Thresholds: Establish specific, measurable weather conditions (wind speeds, lightning proximity, etc.) that automatically trigger predetermined actions.
  • Evacuation Drills: Conduct mandatory evacuation drills for all staff, including security personnel, before the event.
  • Multi-Channel Communication: Implement redundant communication systems for emergency messages to both staff and attendees.
  • Structural Engineering Review: Require third-party engineering certification of all temporary structures with specific attention to weather resilience.
