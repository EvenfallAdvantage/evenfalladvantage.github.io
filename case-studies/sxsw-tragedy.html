South by Southwest Festival Tragedy

2014 Urban Event Security

For Unarmed Event Security Training
Date of Incident: March 13, 2014
Location: Red River Street, Austin, Texas
Event: South by Southwest Festival
Prepared by: James "Fergie" Ferguson
Last Updated: March 31, 2025

Overview

On March 13, 2014, during the South by Southwest (SXSW) Festival in Austin, Texas, a tragic incident unfolded when Rashad Charjuan Owens, a 21-year-old driver fleeing police, plowed his car through a barricade and into a crowd of festival goers on Red River Street. The attack killed four people—two at the scene and two later from injuries—and injured 23 others, leaving a lasting impact on one of the world's largest music, film, and technology festivals. Unlike a natural crowd surge or active shooter scenario, this vehicular assault highlighted unique vulnerabilities at large outdoor events, exposing gaps in security preparedness, barricade effectiveness, and emergency coordination. Owens was convicted of capital murder in 2015 and sentenced to life without parole.

This case study examines the incident from the perspective of unarmed event security, identifying key failures and offering lessons to enhance training and response capabilities for similar high-risk scenarios.

Background

Event Details: SXSW 2014, held from March 7–16, was a sprawling multi-day festival attracting over 300,000 attendees annually to Austin's downtown area. The music portion featured over 2,000 acts across numerous venues, with Red River Street—a hub for nightlife—closed to vehicles and packed with concertgoers.

Security Staffing: Approximately 1,000 security personnel, including unarmed guards from Contemporary Services Corporation (CSC), were deployed alongside Austin Police Department (APD) officers. Exact numbers of unarmed guards are unclear, but they were tasked with crowd control and perimeter security.

Historical Context: SXSW had grown significantly since its 1987 inception, evolving from a niche music showcase into a global event. While prior incidents (e.g., minor crowd disturbances) had occurred, none foreshadowed a vehicular attack of this magnitude.

Timeline of Events

  • Pre-Incident (March 12, 2014): Owens, an aspiring rapper, arrived in Austin with plans to perform at a club. He had a history of minor criminal activity, including a DUI warrant from Alaska.
  • 12:30 AM (March 13): An APD officer attempted to stop Owens at a gas station near 9th Street and Interstate 35 for a traffic violation. Owens fled, driving his gray Honda Civic the wrong way down 9th Street.
  • 12:31 AM: Owens crashed through wooden barricades with metal supports on Red River Street, striking pedestrians outside The Mohawk nightclub. He hit a moped, a bicycle, and multiple people over two blocks, killing Steven Craenmehr (Netherlands) and Jamie Ranae West (Austin) instantly.
  • 12:32 AM: Owens collided with a taxi and a parked van, then fled on foot. Police subdued him with a stun gun and arrested him within minutes.
  • Aftermath: Two more victims, Sandy Thuy Le (March 17) and Deandre Tatum (March 27), died from injuries. Twenty-three others were hospitalized, with five in critical condition initially.

Key Failures in Security and Event Management

Inadequate Training for Unarmed Security:

  • Many unarmed guards were event-specific hires with limited training, often a brief orientation. They lacked preparation for vehicular threats or high-speed pursuits near crowds.
  • No evidence suggests guards were briefed on external risks beyond typical festival concerns (e.g., intoxication, fights).

Insufficient Barricade Design and Placement:

  • The wooden barricades with metal supports were designed for pedestrian control, not to stop a speeding vehicle. Owens easily breached them, exposing a critical vulnerability.
  • Barricades lacked redundancy or sturdier materials (e.g., concrete barriers), common in high-traffic urban events post-9/11.

Poor Coordination and Communication:

  • Unarmed security had no direct line to APD during the pursuit, relying on delayed verbal reports or personal phones. This hindered real-time awareness of the approaching threat.
  • No clear protocol existed for escalating an external incident (e.g., a fleeing suspect) into the festival zone.

Underestimation of External Threats:

  • Security planning focused inward—on crowd behavior within the venue—rather than external risks like a vehicle breaching the perimeter. The festival's open layout and proximity to public roads amplified this oversight.
  • The guard-to-attendee ratio was insufficient for peak hours on a densely packed street like Red River.

Delayed Crowd Response:

  • Unarmed guards and attendees initially mistook the crash for an accident, delaying evacuation efforts. Lack of training in rapid threat recognition left security reactive rather than proactive.

Impact

  • Human Toll: Four deaths and 23 injuries, with lasting trauma for survivors, witnesses, and the Austin community.
  • Legal Consequences: Owens received life without parole in November 2015. Over 20 lawsuits were filed against SXSW, CSC, the City of Austin, and others, alleging negligence. The largest civil case was dismissed in 2017, but settlements followed.
  • Industry Fallout: SXSW implemented stricter permitting, enhanced barricades, and increased security presence in subsequent years. The tragedy fueled debates about the festival's size and safety.

Lessons for Unarmed Event Security

Enhanced Training for Diverse Threats:

  • Train guards to recognize and respond to vehicular threats, not just crowd surges or fights. Include scenarios involving external breaches (e.g., vehicles, pursuits).
  • Conduct pre-event briefings on venue vulnerabilities, such as nearby roads or weak barricades.

Perimeter Security Awareness:

  • Monitor and report suspicious activity beyond the event perimeter, especially near access points. Coordinate with police on active pursuits or threats.
  • Advocate for sturdier barricades (e.g., water-filled barriers, bollards) in high-risk zones.
The SXSW 2014 Tragedy reveals the unpredictable risks facing large-scale events and the vital role unarmed security plays in bridging safety gaps. While guards cannot stop a determined attacker alone, their training, awareness, and coordination can mitigate harm and expedite response.

Recommendations for Event Organizers

  • Risk Assessment: Include vehicular threats in planning, assessing nearby roads and escape routes. Collaborate with police on pursuit protocols.
  • Barricade Standards: Deploy heavy-duty barriers (e.g., concrete, steel) at key access points, tested for impact resistance.
  • Staffing Levels: Increase unarmed security at peak times and zones, aiming for a 1:50 ratio in high-density areas.
  • Real-Time Oversight: Use elevated lookouts or cameras to monitor external approaches, relaying alerts to security teams.
  • Communication Systems: Equip all guards with radios linked to a central command and police, ensuring instant threat reporting.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Train staff in basic triage support and rehearse mass casualty responses with local agencies.
