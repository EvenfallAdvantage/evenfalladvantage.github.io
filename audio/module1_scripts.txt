m1s3 - What is Surveillance?
==============================================
Surveillance is the systematic observation of people, places, or activities for the purpose of gathering information. While surveillance can be conducted for legitimate purposes such as law enforcement or security operations, it can also be used with malicious intent.

==============================================
m1s4 - Types of Surveillance
==============================================
There are three main types of surveillance covered in this course. Physical surveillance involves direct observation using human operatives, either on foot or in vehicles. Technical surveillance uses electronic devices such as cameras, GPS trackers, or listening devices. Cyber surveillance monitors digital activities, social media, emails, and online behavior.

==============================================
m1s5 - What is Stalking?
==============================================
Stalking is a pattern of repeated, unwanted attention and contact that causes fear or concern for safety. According to the U.S. Department of Justice, stalking is defined as "a course of conduct directed at a specific person that would cause a reasonable person to feel fear."

==============================================
m1s6 - Key Elements of Stalking
==============================================
For behavior to be considered stalking, it must meet several key elements. First, there must be a pattern of behavior, typically two or more incidents, though this varies by jurisdiction. Second, the contact must be unwanted - the victim has not consented to the attention. Third, the behavior must cause reasonable fear for safety or substantial emotional distress. Finally, the perpetrator knows or should know their behavior is unwanted.

==============================================
m1s7 - Surveillance vs Stalking
==============================================
While related, surveillance and stalking have important distinctions. Surveillance may be legal when conducted with proper authorization, while stalking is always illegal when it meets statutory requirements. Surveillance is typically covert and professional, whereas stalking may be overt or covert and often escalates over time. Surveillance has an information gathering purpose, while stalking is motivated by harassment, intimidation, or control. Surveillance is conducted by trained operatives, while stalking is conducted by individuals with personal motives.

==============================================
m1s8 - Statistics Part 1
==============================================
The statistics on stalking are sobering. One in six women and one in seventeen men have experienced stalking in their lifetime, according to the CDC. Over seven point five million people are stalked annually in the United States alone.

==============================================
m1s9 - Statistics Part 2
==============================================
Perhaps most alarming, seventy-six percent of femicide victims were stalked prior to being killed. Eighty-five percent of stalking victims know their stalker. And technology-facilitated stalking has increased four hundred percent in recent years.

==============================================
m1s12 - Legal Framework
==============================================
All fifty U.S. states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories have anti-stalking laws. However, definitions and penalties vary by jurisdiction. Federal stalking laws include Title 18 U.S.C. Section 2261A for interstate stalking, Title 18 U.S.C. Section 875(c) for interstate communications containing threats, and Title 47 U.S.C. Section 223 for harassing telephone calls.
